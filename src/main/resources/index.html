Welcome to the Global Health Dashboard

Vaccination has been instrumental in eradicating diseases like smallpox and achieving global herd immunity for others, such as polio. This platform provides comprehensive, unbiased data from the World Health Organisation (WHO) to help families and communities make informed decisions about global immunisation efforts. Whether you are a parent, educator, health professional, or researcher, this site brings together vaccination and infection data in one accessible place.

What you can explore:

  • Explore Data: Filter and analyse vaccination rates by country, vaccine type, region, and time period with interactive charts
  • Trending: Discover infection data patterns across different economic development levels and disease types
  • Insights: View global vaccination coverage on an interactive world map with detailed country-level data
  • Mission Statement: Learn about our target users, project goals, and the team behind this platform

Diseases and vaccines tracked in our database:

Our database contains data on three critical vaccine-preventable diseases and their corresponding vaccines:

  • Measles: A highly contagious viral disease that can cause serious complications and death. Tracked through Measles-containing vaccine coverage data.
  • Pertussis (Whooping Cough): A highly contagious respiratory disease that is particularly dangerous for infants and causes severe coughing fits. Tracked through DTP-containing vaccine coverage data. DTP stands for Diphtheria, Tetanus, and Pertussis.
  • Rubella (German Measles): A viral infection that is particularly dangerous during pregnancy because it can cause severe birth defects in unborn babies. Tracked through Rubella-containing vaccine coverage data.

Why vaccinations matter:

Vaccines save millions of lives every year and are one of the most effective tools in public health. They work by training our immune systems to recognise and fight diseases before we get sick. When enough people in a community are vaccinated, it creates protection for everyone, including those who cannot be vaccinated, such as newborn babies and people with weakened immune systems. This is called herd immunity.

Vaccination programmes have helped eliminate or significantly reduce many deadly diseases worldwide. For example, measles cases have dropped by over 80% globally since 2000 due to vaccination efforts. However, gaps in vaccination coverage can lead to outbreaks, which is why tracking and understanding vaccination data is essential for protecting communities everywhere.
